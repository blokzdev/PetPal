# Threshold and counter-conditioning

The two protocols that work for most leash reactivity are:

1. **Look At That (LAT) / Engage-Disengage.** When the dog notices
   the trigger but hasn't reacted yet (under threshold), mark and
   reward eye contact away from the trigger. The dog learns: trigger
   appears → look at handler → good thing happens. Practice at
   distances where the dog can still take a treat — if they can't
   eat, you're too close.
2. **BAT 2.0 (Behavior Adjustment Training).** The dog moves
   themselves *away* from the trigger as the reward; the user follows
   the dog's choices on a long line. Useful when food-based
   counter-conditioning has plateaued. Less common; the user may
   need a CSAT trainer to learn the timing.

Both protocols share a rule: **always train under threshold.** A
reactive dog cannot learn while flooded. If a session goes over
threshold (the dog reacts), end it, decompress (sniffy walk in a
calm environment), and try again later at a larger working distance.

Common owner mistakes worth flagging:

- **Tightening the leash before the trigger appears.** The dog reads
  the tension and pre-charges. Stay loose; if you have to brace,
  step backward to add distance instead.
- **Using a harness with a back clip on a strong reactive dog.**
  Front-clip harnesses or head halters give the user steering. Not
  prong/e-collars — those add pain at the moment of arousal and
  associate the trigger with pain.
- **Treating prevention like a setback.** Crossing the street to
  avoid an approaching dog is *the protocol working*. The dog stays
  under threshold, the user manages distance, no rehearsal of the
  reactive pattern.
