# Introducing a new cat

Most failed multi-cat households are introductions that went too
fast. The standard protocol is **slow and reversible**:

1. **Separate space, days 1–7.** New cat in a closed room with food,
   water, litter, hiding spots, vertical perches. No visual contact
   with resident cats. The new cat is decompressing from the move;
   the resident cats are processing the smell of a stranger.
2. **Scent swap, days 3–10.** Trade bedding, feed both cats on
   either side of the closed door, rub a sock on each cat and place
   it in the other's space. The goal is "I know there's another cat,
   they're around, no one is dying."
3. **Visual access through a barrier, days 7–21.** Cracked door + a
   baby gate stack, a screen, the slats of a closet door. Both cats
   eat tasty food in sight of each other but cannot make contact.
   Hissing is OK; sustained staring or freezing is too much — back
   off a stage.
4. **Supervised time together, weeks 2–6.** Short sessions, ending
   *before* anyone goes over threshold. Lots of vertical space,
   multiple exits, treats. End on calm, not on conflict.
5. **Unsupervised, when truly calm.** Gauge by the boring stuff —
   they nap on the same couch, walk past each other without freezing.

**Restart, don't push.** If a stage goes badly — sustained hissing,
swatting, one cat hiding for hours after — back up two stages, give
it a few more days, try again. Pushing forward through tension
*teaches* the cats that being near each other is unpleasant.

**Timeline expectations.** A successful slow introduction is 2–6
weeks. Households that rush this often spend the next 18 months
managing a chronic tension situation. The slow path is the fast path.

If the user describes the introduction "going badly," resist the
urge to give a single magic-bullet tip. Walk them backward through
the stages, find where it derailed, and propose a restart.
