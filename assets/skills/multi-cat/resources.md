# Resources, access, and the n+1 rule

Most ongoing multi-cat tension is a resource access problem, not a
personality clash. Cats time-share territory; they need the option
to use a resource without having to negotiate for it.

**Litter boxes.** The rule is **n+1** — for two cats, three boxes;
for three cats, four. Spread across the home, ideally on multiple
floors. Avoid placing boxes in corridors or corners where one cat
can be ambushed on the way out. If a cat is peeing just outside the
box, that's often the message "another cat is guarding this one,"
not a training issue.

**Food and water stations.** Plural, and not next to each other. If
two cats have to eat shoulder-to-shoulder at one bowl, the more
assertive cat eats faster, the other eats less, and both stay mildly
stressed. Multiple feeding stations also make medication-via-food
possible without the wrong cat eating the dose.

**Vertical space.** Cats reduce conflict by using the third
dimension. Cat trees, shelves, the back of the sofa, the top of the
fridge — these let one cat be "above" the other without either
having to leave the room. A multi-cat home with no climbing options
forces all conflict-resolution onto floor-level posturing.

**Hiding spots.** Each cat needs at least one place where the other
cats cannot follow. Cardboard boxes, under-bed access, a cat tree
with an enclosed cubby. The cat who's getting the worst of the
household dynamics needs a guaranteed retreat that they don't have
to defend.

**Resource guarding signs to flag.** One cat sleeping on top of the
litter box. One cat consistently positioned in the doorway between
another cat and the food. One cat who only eats at night when the
other is asleep. None of these involve obvious fighting; all of
them mean the household is running tight.

When the user describes any of these, prompt them to log it with
`write_wiki_entry` and start counting boxes / feeding stations /
vertical perches against the n+1 rule. Adding resources is the
cheapest intervention in cat behavior.
