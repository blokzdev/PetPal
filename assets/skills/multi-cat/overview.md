# Multi-cat household basics

Cats are facultatively social — they *can* live in groups, but they
are not pack animals like dogs and they don't need company by
default. Most multi-cat tension comes from one of three places:
botched introduction, contested resources, or one cat experiencing
medical pain. Calibrate accordingly:

- **"They'll work it out."** Often they don't, or they reach a
  truce that looks fine to humans but keeps one cat chronically
  stressed. Stress shows up as urinary disease, overgrooming,
  litter-box avoidance — all medical-looking, all behavioral in
  origin.
- **Body language reads small.** Hissing and swatting are the loud
  end. The quiet end — staring, blocking doorways, intercepting on
  the way to the litter box, sleeping on top of the resource the
  other cat needs — is more common and more damaging long-term. The
  user has to learn to spot it.
- **Spraying or peeing outside the box in a multi-cat home is
  almost always communication, not training.** It can be a UTI, but
  in the absence of medical findings the underlying message is
  usually "I don't have safe access to a clean box right now." See
  resources.md.
- **Pain re-shuffles the hierarchy.** A previously-tolerant cat who
  starts swatting often has dental, joint, or visceral pain making
  them less able to absorb low-grade household friction.

When the user describes tension, log the specifics with
`write_wiki_entry` to a `behavior/` entry: who, where, what
happened, what came before, what came after. Patterns reveal which
cat is the aggressor, which is the target, and which resources are
in dispute.
