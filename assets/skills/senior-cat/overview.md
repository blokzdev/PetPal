# Senior cat basics

Cats hide illness — it's a survival reflex, and it works against
their owners. By the time a senior cat looks "off," the underlying
condition is often well-established. Calibrate accordingly:

- **"Senior" starts younger than people expect.** AAFP guidelines
  put it at 11+; "geriatric" at 15+. Indoor cats routinely live to
  18–20 with good preventive care.
- **Twice-yearly vet visits are the standard.** Annual is too sparse
  for senior cats — six months is roughly the equivalent of a year
  in terms of disease progression. Baseline blood work + urinalysis
  catch the big three (kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, diabetes)
  before symptoms force the issue.
- **Weight is the single most useful number.** Track it monthly. A
  10% drop in three months — or a quiet 0.5 kg loss in a 4 kg cat
  — is meaningful even if everything else looks fine.
- **Behavior changes are medical until proven otherwise.** Sudden
  vocalization at night, aggression toward another cat, missing
  the litter box, hiding more — these read as "old age" but are
  more often pain, cognitive change, or undiagnosed disease.

When the user mentions weight, water intake, litter-box changes, or
new behaviors, use `write_wiki_entry` to log specifics with date.
The trends matter; one snapshot doesn't.
