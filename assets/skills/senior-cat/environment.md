# Environment for an aging cat

Most senior-cat quality-of-life work happens at home, between vet
visits. The right small environmental changes prevent pain and the
litter-box accidents that pain quietly causes.

**Litter box.** Low entry — under 4 inches if the cat is hesitating
to step in. Larger surface area than they'd need at age 5. One box
on every floor of the house, plus n+1 (three boxes for two cats —
see multi-cat pack). Unscented clumping; senior cats often develop
fragrance sensitivity.

**Resting and sleeping.** Soft, warm, easy to climb into. Heated
pet beds are appropriate for arthritic seniors in cool homes.
Multiple options at multiple heights — the cat self-selects what
their joints can tolerate that day.

**Vertical space access.** A cat who used to perch on the
bookcase but stopped didn't lose interest — they lost the jump.
Pet stairs, ramps, an intermediate hop point (a stool next to the
sofa, a chair next to the bed) restore access without forcing the
joints.

**Food and water access.** Multiple stations spread around the
house, especially across floors. An aging cat who's stiff in the
morning shouldn't have to navigate stairs to get water. Raised
bowls help cats with neck arthritis or dental pain.

**Routine.** Senior cats handle change worse than middle-aged
cats. New furniture layouts, new pets, schedule shifts — all can
trigger litter-box lapses or hiding. Pre-empting the change with
gradual introduction (Feliway, scent-swap, slow rearrangement)
avoids weeks of stress.

When the user describes hesitation around stairs, missing the box,
or stopped jumping, prompt them to log the date with
`write_wiki_entry` to a `behavior/` entry — the timeline matters
when they next see the vet.
